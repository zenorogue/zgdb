GAME Diablo
BY Blizzard
VAL year=1997 played=2000 xp=3 pc helltheme realtime grid isometric hscroll vscroll xp=3 gem=1 diagrid rpgequip rpgconsume rpgstats rpgquests bossfights photorealistic procgen randomized multiplayer return nonlinear exp combat manualsave bossfights difflevels

Diablo is usually credited as an inventor of the ARPG genre, but it should be called Action Roguelike instead,
as the first extremely successful game that was very similar to roguelikes (specifically {Angband}) but replaced
the deep turn-based gameplay of roguelikes with action. There were isometric action RPGs before Diablo, for
example I have played {Son of the Empire} before it (I also played {FTA2} but that was after-Diablo).
People who knew what roguelikes were called Diablo roguelike, it was also one of the "roguelikes" that
Edmund McMillen played before creating {The Binding of Isaac}; but after that game was created,
the buzzword understanding was all about permadeath, and the roguelike community
became more strict on turn-based games. (And permadeath was in fact intended -- I have played without permadeath,
so I cannot give xp=4 for it). I like Diablo more than {Diablo II}.

GAME Iji
BY Daniel Remar
BY country: Sweden
VAL year=2008 played=2009 free realtime platformer sideview hscroll vscroll return female scifi pc xp=4 gem=3 multiweapon fixed vectorart free bossfights ammolimit=? rulediscovery ability secrets
In my opinion, this freeware platformer gem is best played if you do not know what to expect, so I will not explain why I love this game.
I have downloaded it in the evening played it for the whole night, and then, wanted to play it again immediately.
Anyway, quite an interesting take on the metroidvania-ish feature of backtracking -- the structure is linear, but if you are like me,
you will want to play it again to find the secrets you missed.

SPOILER
The most interesting thing about the game is its subversion of typical player expectations. In a typical RPG you kill
all the monsters for XP. You can do this in Iji too, but if you do, it will make you question your choice --
the game reminds you via its 'logs' that maybe killing so many monsters means that you are not actually that
a good person, and also it is easy to notice that killing these monsters were indeed your choice. So after
playing the game for the first game like that, I wanted to play again as a pacifist, and the game was much
nicer that way. Especially that you know that what you have is essentially the best weapon in the known
universe and you choose not to use it for moral reasons... There are also other subversions, like parodies
of the "completionist" approach where you do everything possible in a game.

GAME Mortuum
VAL year=1995 played=1998 topdown slasher xp=4 gem=2 physics medieval pc fixed twistpixel seq multiweapon realtime
A topdown arcade game about a guy slaying enemies with their sword (or later, other weapons), made using Klik & Play.
It definitely counts as a hidden gem -- I do not hear anyone talking about it, but I had great fun with it
myself. What made it interesting was the physics-based weapon mechanics -- you did the most damage not by simply
sticking your weapons into the enemies, but by swinging them so that it had lots of momentum.

GAME Civilization
BY Sid Meier
BY MicroProse
VAL year=1991 played=1993 pc grid turnbased xxxx xp=4 hscroll vscroll history randomized nonlinear procgen hexgrid topdown lores economy army manualsave difflevels
This is of course a famous series of games that everybody should play. 
The classic 4X game, I have played it quite a lot. Everyone knows it, so not much point to talk much about it.

I have found some interesting bugs that do not
seem talked about: if you had two ships you could abuse the mechanics of 'unload' command giving full movement to all sentry units to travel as
far as you want in a single turn; and you could also do Settlers work in a single turn. (Somehow lots of people talk about the 'nuclear Gandhi'
bug, which apparently is just an urban legend.)

It uses a distinctive graphical style that makes the game look similar to board games: units have rectangles in the backgrounds which tell
which player they belong to. It fell out of fashion, mostly all current games use a more "realistic" graphical style where units
are just superimposed over the background terrain, and you need to look at the colors of their uniforms to tell the side. But I think it was cool.

GAME Son of the Empire
VAL year=1993 played=1996 isometric gem=2 xp=4 engineering medieval lores fixed flick overworld rpgstats rpgequip rpgconsume pauseable nonlinear intrapower
I think this should be more well-known because of being an isometric action RPG, well before {Diablo}. 
You control a team of 4 characters, mostly going through dungeons, fighting monsters and collecting loot;
also an overworld map where they could visit further dungeons and towns to sell/buy stuff, or fight armies.
A very interesting spellcasting system where you could construct spells out of components, for example
Surround-Missile-Paralyze to shoot a paralyzing missile in each of 8 directions; this is the model of
"programmable magic" to me, instead of {Magicka} or {Noita}. The dungeons have some puzzles where you
need to cast a correct combination of runes so that you reach specific spots with a specific spell.
Otherwise than being an isometric action RPG, not very similar to Diablo, for example, the dungeons
are not randomized. This is a sequel to {Legend}.

GAME Lemmings II: The Tribes
BY DMA Design
VAL pc year=1993 played=1994 weirdtheme animaltheme hscroll vscroll destructible pixelsim xp=4 sideview platformer fixed lores nonlinear pauseable army traps=?
A sequel to {Lemmings}. While in Lemmings we had mixed levels with graphics of various themes, 
here we have 12 separate 'tribes', each with its own theme. There are lots of abilities, instead of just 10 from the
original, and also the levels are larger. In each tribe, the number of lemmings you have equals the number of lemmings saved in the previous level,
and to win the whole game, you have to save most lemmings in every tribe; I do not think this was a good design
decision -- if you solve a level poorly, you can continue with only one lemming, but not much point to do that since
you will have to redo these levels anyway. Independent levels are better for such puzzles.

GAME Craft
VAL pc year=1997 played=1998 medieval army realtime lores hscroll vscroll procgen xp=2 topdown multiplayer exp combat gem=1 randomized
This game seems to be hard to find nowadays... blame the extremely unspecific name, I guess? It was an open source RTS game that could be found
in Debian Linux software repositories in the 90s. It was a very simple RTS with a few units of usual kinds: peasants, swordmen, archers, and catapults.
However it had some interesting features that were not in other RTS games of this time. You could put your units on ships, and then they would
affect the military power of the ship in obvious ways (catapults and archers would shoot, swordsmen would attack when trying to board an enemy ship).
The peasants could increase or decrease the height of fragments of the map, you could use that to create fortifications or moats. Unfortunately, while
I found these aspect interesting, it was not really possible to experience them (no predesigned scenarios where you attacked enemy forts or defended
yourself, only a very basic AI that did nothing of these things). You could play against a human of course, but I have not tried to convince anyone

the later stages of {The Witness}, but Perspective was earlier. (There are also other similarities, like pipes which connect the various puzzles and lighten up and continue to the
next puzzle when the current one is solved -- I guess The Witness took inspiration, or maybe they both took inspiration from the same source.) Very cool, more fun than The Witness IMO,
even if short. I know that the devs later created the game {Superliminal}.
